When a Partner Dies in Switzerland: The Complete Financial Picture

📅 Updated March 2026 ⏱ 11 min read 🇨🇭 Swiss law 2025/2026

The death of a partner is devastating on every level. But families who have thought through the financial implications ahead of time are in a far better position to focus on what matters — grief, recovery, and moving forward — without the added burden of financial shock. This article explains exactly what happens to a Swiss family's income and expenses when one partner dies.

The Income Side: What Replaces the Lost Salary?

In Switzerland, three sources of income kick in immediately after the death of a working partner:

1. AHV (Pillar 1) Survivor Pensions

The AHV widow/widower pension begins automatically. You do not need to apply urgently — the compensation office is notified by the civil registry. You should apply to your cantonal Ausgleichskasse as soon as possible. Benefits can be backdated up to 5 years under Swiss law, but applying promptly avoids complications.

  • Widow/widower pension: 80% of the deceased's AHV retirement pension, up to CHF 2,016/month
  • Orphan pension: 40% per child, up to age 18 (or 25 in education)
  • These are paid monthly, indexed to inflation, for life (widow/widower) or until the child is of age

2. BVG (Pillar 2) Partner Pension

The pension fund pays the partner pension according to the rules of the specific fund (at least 60% of the disability pension under BVG minimum). Importantly:

  • Paid monthly for life, typically without inflation adjustment
  • May stop if the surviving partner remarries (check your fund rules)
  • If you are cohabiting but not married, you may not be automatically entitled — check your fund rules and register your partner explicitly

3. Lump Sum Payouts

The following are paid as one-time lump sums shortly after death:

  • Pillar 3a balance or death capital (if insurance 3a)
  • Term life insurance (Pillar 3b) death sum
  • BVG death capital if no pension is payable (e.g. single with no children)
  • Inheritance — savings, investments, property

The Expense Side: What Changes After Death?

One of the most counterintuitive aspects of financial planning for death is that expenses don't fall by as much as people expect. Here is what actually changes:

What stops immediately

  • The deceased's personal spending (mobile phone, personal transport, individual clothing, personal subscriptions)
  • The deceased's health insurance premium (the surviving adult's portion continues, plus children's)
  • The deceased's Pillar 3a and term insurance premiums
  • Mortgage amortisation payments — if the lump sum is used to pay off the mortgage

What continues unchanged

  • Rent or housing costs (if no mortgage to pay off)
  • Utilities — electricity, water, heating do not halve when one person dies
  • Food — reduces, but not by 50%
  • Children's costs — school, activities, medical — continue fully
  • Communication — internet, TV licence, the survivor's phone continue
  • Insurance premiums — car, liability, legal, REGA continue
  • Taxes — may reduce if income falls into a lower bracket

New costs that appear

  • Extra childcare — if both partners were working and one was informally providing childcare, paid childcare costs increase significantly. In Switzerland: CHF 1,500–2,500/child/month for full-time Krippe.
  • Household help — tasks the deceased partner handled (cooking, cleaning, administration) may require paid help
  • Mortgage interest — if the mortgage is not paid off, ongoing interest continues (though amortisation might stop if lump sum pays down the principal)

The Monthly Gap: A Worked Example

Let's trace through a realistic Swiss family scenario:

Family setup

Partner 1 (P1): CHF 110,000/year gross, net CHF 7,700/month. Partner 2 (P2): CHF 70,000/year, net CHF 5,200/month. Two children aged 4 and 7. Mortgage: CHF 700,000 at 1.8%.

Monthly expenses today: CHF 12,500/month.

Scenario: P1 dies.

New monthly income:

  • AHV widow pension: CHF 2,016
  • AHV orphan pensions (2 × CHF 1,008): CHF 2,016
  • BVG partner pension (estimate): CHF 1,650
  • P2 salary: CHF 5,200
  • Total: CHF 10,882/month

Adjusted monthly expenses (mortgage paid off from lump sum, P1's health share removed, P1's phone/personal removed, but +CHF 2,000/month extra childcare):

  • Expenses drop from CHF 12,500 → CHF 10,800 (approx, mortgage gone, some savings)
  • Plus extra childcare: +CHF 2,000
  • Total expenses: CHF 12,800/month

Monthly gap: CHF 12,800 − CHF 10,882 = CHF 1,918/month

Without term life insurance, this gap would erode the family's savings at nearly CHF 2,000/month. Over 20 years until both children finish education, that's CHF 460,000 needed from the buffer — in addition to the mortgage that was already paid off.

How Long Does the Buffer Last?

The "buffer" is the net cash available after paying off the mortgage: Pillar 3a capital + term insurance payout + existing savings − mortgage.

If the buffer is CHF 200,000 and the monthly gap is CHF 1,918:

  • Buffer ÷ gap = 104 months = 8.7 years

After 8.7 years, the surviving partner has depleted all reserves and is running purely on monthly income — which still doesn't cover expenses. This is the financial reality that term life insurance is designed to prevent.

What the Surviving Partner Should Do Immediately

In the weeks after the death of a partner, these financial steps matter:

  1. Register the death at the civil registry — triggers AHV and BVG notifications automatically
  2. Apply to the cantonal Ausgleichskasse for AHV widow/widower and orphan pensions
  3. Contact the employer's pension fund for BVG partner and orphan pension
  4. Notify the Pillar 3a insurer or bank to release the death capital
  5. File the life insurance claim (Pillar 3b term policy)
  6. Review the mortgage with the bank — options include paying off with lump sum or restructuring
  7. Consult a Vermögensverwaltung or fee-only financial advisor to plan the new financial reality

⚠️ Time limits matter: AHV orphan pension applications should be made within 5 years of the death to avoid losing backdated payments. BVG and insurance claims typically need to be made promptly. Keep all certificates and policy documents accessible.
